Study of Degas: Ballerinas and the Art Process

The Artist: Edgar Degas

Edgar Degas was born as Hilaire-Germain-Edgar De Gas on July 19 1863 in Paris, France. He is regarded as one of the founders of the Impessionist Movement, though he disliked the term and prefered to be called a realist. And unlike many of the Impressionists of that time he did not paint outdoors. He prefered to paint indoors relying on photos, memory or live models.

Degas was a superb draftsman and in particular was a master of depicting movement. This is seen in many of this subject matters depicting dancers, horses, racejockies, women bathing. He worked in several mediums such as oil paint, pastels, charcol, etching, monotype, wax scuplting, and sometimes bronze casting.

As we look at art works by Degas we will go over the draftsman ship and process he took to create his completed works of art.

The Dance Class

The Dance Class depicts some 24 Ballerinas with their mothers waiting during an examination of a ballerina in the center of the painting being conducted by ballet master, Jules Perrot (gentleman on the right side of the image). This painting has an uptilted perspective that rushes us into the scene. Degas would change and shift dancers in this scene as he worked. He would sketch figures in various positions and when then adding them to the image he would then shift the figures or add figures to suit the composition. Even the scene depicted in the paiting is of imaginary set rehearsal room in the old Paris Opera which had recently burned down during the time of this painting.

The Little Fourteen-Year-Old Dancer

Upon Degas's death in 1917, more than 150 figurative sculptures were found in his studio. Most were made of fragile wax, clay, and plastiline (a wax- and oil-based modeling material). Many had deteriorated. Only a some of these sculptures were preserved in copies cast in plaster. Although Degas did not favor reproducing his sculptures in more permanent materials, his heirs authorized copies to be cast in bronze in order to preserve the compositions and to sell them as finished works. Degas was self taught in sculpture and made numerous wax models on wire armatures. The subjects were dancers, women bathing, and horses just like his subjects in his paintings, but the sculptural studies are not preparatory for specific oil canvases. They were more for independent explorations of three-dimensional form. He would show the wac scupltures to friends and ordered plaster casts of three of them during his lifetime, so their creation was not an entirely private activity. But the only one he ever exhibited in public was the wax version of Little Fourteen-Year-Old Dancer. Degas made the sculpture of tinted wax with real cloth, tulle, and ribbon, and the result reminded many viewers of the displays in Madame Tussaud’s wax museum in London.

Degas’s model was the young Marie van Goethem (born June 7, 1865), a student at the École de Danse and by 1880 a professional dancer at the Paris Opéra. Degas’s sketches of Marie indicate his intense concentration on her form. The artist would frequent the Opéra, making endless studies of dancers in motion and at rest. In the bronze, shown below model is in a classic ballet stance, with her right leg advanced and turned. But there is a deliberate awkwardness to the pose in the way that the arms jut down behind and the head tilts back. In addition many thought of the features of this adolescent were far from pretty.
